<div class="column-right">
<p>A few years back, Fray David Moreno and his siblings split up their family farm that was started in La Despensa valley of Urrao some 50 years ago. He named his small plot, "El Rosal" (the rose bush) and re-planted the entire plot in Caturra Chiroso, a natural mutation of Caturra (possible regression) that is a unique heirloom found in the Urrao region. Chiroso has an elongated seed that looks a lot like Typica and we've found that it tends to have unique and favorible cup qualities. Finca El Rosal is roughly 1 hectare in size (about 2 acres) and sits at 1900 meters above sea level. High altitude is the norm around here as the valley floor in Urrao is 1850 meters at the low point! One way small holders manage processing tiny day-lots (what they can pick in a day) during the harvest is by mixing cherry over the course of a few days in the fermentation tank. They can get away with what is thought to be a "no no" in the industry, partly because of the access to cold ground water and colder temperatures in general. These factors slow fermentation, and changing out the water daily helps too. But the longer fermentation time does tend to impart some fruity flavors that are more a product of the process rather than inherent to the seed. This coffee is a good example of that affect on the cup and really turned out nicely.</p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>This lot from Fray David Moreno is another in a string of fruit-forward coffees from the Southwestern Antioquian town of Urrao. The fragrance and aroma are berry-toned, with allusions of berry pancake syrup, blackberry compote and the like. The level of sweetness matches these intense fruited characteristics, especially in the wet aroma. The fruited notes in the cup have a tangy-sweet flavor compound that reminds me of pomegranate and balsamic vinegar reduction. I think the cleanliness suffers a little bit in City roasts, but the tradeoff is a much more tart acidic impression in the cup. At City+, the sweetness captures flavors of pure brown sugar, like panela and piloncillo. The coffee cools to a grape accent note that moves into lingering bittersweetness in the finish. </p> </div>
