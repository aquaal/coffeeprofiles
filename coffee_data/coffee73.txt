<div class="column-right">
<p>This is a unique year for Ethiopian coffee exports as it's the first where farmers like Mohammed Haji Aba Simel were given the opportunity to export their own coffee. In previous years, all private operations big and small had to sell their coffee through the government run Ethiopia Commodity Exchange and only cooperatives and unions were exempt. One issue that presented to the buyer is that ECX coffee was stripped of provenance, save origin, so we never knew where it came from. Mohammed's coffee, for example, would've just been blended off with other coffees of a similar grade and sold as "Jimma Grade 3". All that has changed and he is one of many farmers who have successfully applied for export licenses and are able to sell directly. His farm and wet mill are located near Agaro in Beshasha village, hi coffee planted at 1900 meters above sea level. Mohammed Haji Aba Simel is one of a few single-farmer lots we were lucky enough to export directly this year. We hope to repeat this business in 2020!</p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>Both the fragrance and wet aromatics are plump with berry smells, like blueberry and strawberry jams when roasted light, chocolate-covered berry at Full City. The flavors in the brewed coffee however, in many ways remind me of a honey process coffee rather than natural. Aba Simel doesn't have the rustic characteristics I would expect from a DP Limu, but rather, light roasts yield a tart cup with somewhat refined fruited flavors. Apricot and orange come to mind as I work my way through the cup, an orange marmalade flavor with a touch of tartness. Acidity is very pleasant, especially considering process. The light roasts cool to flavors of unrefined sugar and a lemon note. My Full City roast was darn near 2nd cracks (FC+) and showing oils on the surface by 2nd day of resting. Bittersweet roast tones are front and center, with dark berry and prune flavors notes at the lead, but fading in the aftertaste. All in all and interesting dry process coffee from a part of Ethiopia where we tend to stick with wet process. The one unfortunate aspect to note is we only have 11 bags. Get some while you can.</p> </div>
