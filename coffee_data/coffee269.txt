<div class="column-right">
<p>The Frinsa Estate is a family run farm and export company in the Bandung highlands region of West Java. The farm was set up in 2011 and planted in mostly Ateng types, a Catimor commonly planted in Indonesia. Altitude ranges from 1300 to 1600 meters above sea level and they're producing both fully washed and wet hulled process types. This is the latter, also called "<a href="https://legacy.sweetmarias.com/library/why-should-you-know-giling-basah">giling basah</a>", and tends to produce bodied, low acid coffees, also notable for earthy tones like this wet-hulled lot. We found this coffee excelled when roasted to Full City and beyond, bringing out the cocoa bittersweets as well as complex earth and herbal accent notes. It would work really well as a Moka-Java blend base using one of our dry process Ethiopias 50/50, or a dry process coffee from Rwanda or Burundi. Frinsa Estate has their own wet mill, dry mill and warehouse storage facilities and able to monitor quality control from the initial cherry harvesting all the way down to sorting defects from processed green beans. </p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>This wet hulled Java has an earth-toned aroma not unlike Sumatra. The smells are sweet and marked by rustic syrups and smokey cocoa roast, noting date sugar and buttery caramel when we cupped it, but also with compost-like earthy aroma that often come with wet hulling. Brewed Full City roasts have opaque body and intense wet-hulled cup flavors. Rustic sweeteners like palm sugar most accurately describe the sweetness and are closely shadowed by a persistent bittering cacao flavor. Smoked chocolate and peat come together in an earthy, bittersweet bass note, accented by complex hints of tobacco, green bell pepper, and citrus rind. This coffee excels as dark roast and I took one of my roast tests a full minute into 2nd snaps (FC++). Second Crack brings out smokey aromatics like dark-roasted cacao and charred cedar, carbonized sugar, anise and a patch of pipe tobacco accenting the finish. This is definitely a single origin espresso option and Frinsa's inky body and complex bittersweets will work well as a blend base too. I'd like to try equal ratios with a dry process Ethiopian or East African coffee for a Mocha-Java type espresso blend. </p> </div>
