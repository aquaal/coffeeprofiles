<div class="column-right">
<p>This coffee is the product of a newly-started coffee cherry buying operation in the Haupu tribal region of Timor Leste. The project was set in motion by Australian native Peter Dougan who developed a farm fresh produce program in East Timor a few years back. He's since added coffee to the mix, partly as a way to boost farmer's incomes, which has developed into something more substantial over the past few years. A wet mill was erected on land owned by a local leader and the buying operation began. The wet mill is called "Lutlala", also the name of the hamlet within which it located, and is owned by a local leader. This lot comes from farms near the village of Lacau in the Ermera District, near the slopes of mount Ramelau. The farms span a range of 1500 - 1700 meters above sea level and you mostly see Timor hybrids and Typica cultivars in this region. This is a honey process coffee, a first from Timor for us. As you'll see in the review, it's not a particularly fruit-forward variety of honey, expressiong more of a clean sweetness and slight nuanced aromatics. But put it next to our regular, washed <a href="https://www.sweetmarias.com/timor-leste-dukurai-daurfusu-6435.html">Timor Dukurai Darfusu</a> that's also available right now and those nuances really stand out. A fun opportunity to compare and contrast the two coffees from neighboring communities. </p> </div>

<p>There was quite a contrast between this honey process Timor and a fully washed El Salvador I cupped side by side. With or without the context of the balanced El Salvadorian coffee, I wouldn't call this coffee "fruity" like some other honeys we've carried, but it certainly shows fruited accents. The smells are mild and clean, with notes of raw sugar, vanilla wafer and pistachio ice cream. The cup is where fruited hints come into play. Light roasts show a subtle citrus note that lends to the acidic impression. The sweetness is on the simple side, like table sugar, and rounds out flavor notes of black tea, lemon zest and a dried apple note. Steer clear of roasting dark. City and City+ roasts shine as pour-over brew and I imagine will have more rounded sweetness and body if brewed as French Press. Steer clear of roasting too dark though. My Full City+ roast tasted ashy and was unpalatable in my opinion.</p> </div>
