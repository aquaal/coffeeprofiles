<div class="column-right">
<p>This custom decaf is from the Bitta farm in Bonga forest in Ethiopia's Kafa Zone. The Bonga forest is an amazing and lush natural forest reserve and fabled as the birthplace of coffee. Bitta farm is roughly 1000 hectares in size and planted in local indigenous types in a semi-forested area with some of the primary canopy still intact. The farm spans an altitude range of 1800 to 1950 meters above sea level. The majority of the coffee that went into this decaf was wet-processed, though we did add about 1/5 dry process to help amplify fruited flavors and sweetness. We find that a small amount of dry process coffee makes a huge difference in the final decaf product, no doubt lending to the "un-decaf" cup profile. Take care to watch for physical signs of fracturing during the roast as 1st crack is super quiet. We almost missed it altogether! The coffee also takes on a fairly dark hue when roasting and may appear darker than it actually is. As stated in the review, it can be bit tricky to roast, but very rewarding for its sweetness - and the fact it really doesn't have a "decaf-y" flavor at all!</p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>I feel like we say this often, but this lot in terms of flavor is truly about as "un-decaf" as they come. Cupping after 24 hours rest was a real treat and revisiting the brew over the course of a weekend reinforced this notion. The dry fragrance has strong chocolate notes with molasses undertones, burned sugar and stone fruit accents. Brown sugars are emphasized in the wet aroma, with a fruited aspect of dried tamarind and warming spice accents the steam when running my spoon through the wetted crust. Impressive sweetness is found in the brewed coffee and flavors come off like peach caramelized with brown sugar. The cup is filled with fruit and unrefined sugars, sweet spice like cinnamon and chai and mild acidity (another reason it stands out against other decafs). I should point out that first crack is very hard to hear, and color looks much darker relative to degree of roast. A bit tricky to roast, but very rewarding for its sweetness - and the fact it really doesn't have a "decaf-y" flavor at all!</p> </div>
