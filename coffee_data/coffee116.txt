<div class="column-right">
<p>Kiruga is another wet mill under the Othaya Farmer's Cooperative Society (FCS) umbrella. Othaya has 18 wet mills, or what they call "Factories" in Kenya, and this particular cooperative society dates back to the late 1950s. "Factories" are essentially small washing stations aligned with a cooperative society in Kenya. We return to the societies who seem to regularly produce some of the best Kenya coffees, and each year we come across societies that are new to us as well. Being one of the older Kenya FCS, we are well aware of the quality coming out of Othaya, and have had the good fortune to buy a few stellar lots the past few years now. The processing site sits just above 1800 meters above sea level, and the farmer members are growing SL-28, SL-34 and Ruiru-11 cultivars at nearby farms between 1800 and 2000 meters. This is the AA outturn, the largest bean size in Kenya coffee grades and commands the highest price. Every process batch is made up of a fairly wide range of different sized beans, and in Kenya these sizes are grouped together by range measured in 1/16ths of an inch. These different classifications are generally referred to as "outturns", the most common being "AA" = 17-19 1/64", "AB" = 15-17 1/64", and peaberry.</p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>This AA lot from the Kiruga cooperative produces a clean, bright cup, shimmering citrus-like vibrance when roasted light that takes a turn toward fruits of a darker hue in more of a medium, Full City roast level. The fragrance of light roasts has raw sugar sweetness, a hint of lemon peel and baking spices. Pouring the hot water releases a smell of honey-lemon tea in the steam. My Full City roast showed some of the usual roast bittering smells, but dark berry notes were pervasive as well. The sweetness in the cup is fairly transparent, somewhere between table (white/refined) and light brown sugar, adding dimension the the cup flavors. This is definitely a bright coffee (9.4!) and City roasts have lemon-like acidity that really pops. The cup cools to sweet flavors that have some fruited characteristics, something like lemonade with a hint of Botan rice candies and hop florals in the nose. Full City roasts are very nice, and while not always recommended for Kenya, really suit Kiruga. The cup is chocolatey without being too bitter, some of that having to do with the confluence of roast bittering and caramelized sweetness. A fruited note is apparent as the cup cools down some and comes off like blueberry.</p> </div>
