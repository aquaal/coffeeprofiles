<div class="column-right">
<p>This is a small lot is built from the coffees of a few small producers in the El Paraiso micro-region of Huehuetenango. This lush, high-land valley boasts a dramatic landscape of jutting cliffs and seemingly endless green as far as you can see. Farmers in the region, grow coffee on small parcels of land often adjacent to where they live - in many cases less than one hectare of coffee. Bourbon and Caturra are dominant varietals, but you'll see the occasional Catuaí and Typica trees mixed in. Processing is managed locally, each farmer de-pulping and fermenting their coffee at home, or at a neighbor's, then laying it out to dry on concrete patios. Slight variations in processing technic can make for unique cup profiles from one neighbor's coffee to the next. The dry parchment coffee is transported to a dry milling facility in Huehuetenango town where it is hulled, sorted and prepared for export. </p> </div>

<p>This custom Xinabajul blend kicks things off with a buttery, toffee-coated nut smell in the dry fragrance, accents that hint at cinnamon and malted chocolate milk balls. Adding hot water pulls out a butterscotch almond note in the steam, along with dark caramel and subtle chocolate note. City+ roasts produce a sweet undercurrent of raw sugar and caramel flavors that lay the groundwork for a crowd pleasing coffee. As you move through the cup, any fruited accents that come through are mild. "Raisin" was the single fruited note jotted down in our cupping log, but plays second fiddle to developed, caramel-like sweet flavors in the brew. Notes of caramel flan, blonde brownie and vanilla extract underscore this aspect of the cup. I was also surprised somewhat citric acidity when roasted to City/City+ roast levels. This coffee excels at Full City roast level too, presenting bold dark cocoa flavors and heavy, inky body. Espresso shots are bittersweet and have an aromatic licorice hint.</p> </div>
