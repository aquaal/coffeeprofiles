<div class="column-right">
<p>DiRahmah started a coffee cooperative in Bebesen district of Aceh 12 years ago, and since it's inception, has grown to serve small-holder coffee farmers in 19 different villages in Bebesen. This lot represents a blend of those farmers, whose coffee farms span an altitude range of about 1300 to 1600 meters above sea level, and are planted in older, longberry types (you'll see the long, canoe shaped beans) like Ateng and Tim Tim. Rahmah's cooperative incentivizes membership by offering agronomical assistance, organic fertilizers, seedlings, and of course, paying well above the local Aceh prices most other collectors are paying. The cooperative membership currently boasts 1500 strong. This is a wet hulled coffee, the traditional process method in Sumatra. The name "wet hulled", comes from the fact that the fruit and parchment layer are stripped from the green coffee beans while they are still wet. In most other growing regions, the parchment layer is left intact during drying and acts as a sort of protective barrier for the seed from the outside elements. It's what leads to the rustic profile that Sumatran coffee is known for. Rahmah is able to control this process to some extent by building drying rooms where the coffee is protected to some extent from the fluctuating climate as the intermal moisture inside the bean comes down to a more stable level. Read more about this incredibly unique process method in our blog post, "<a href="https://library.sweetmarias.com/why-should-you-know-giling-basah/">Why Should Know Giling Basah?</a>"</p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>Right from the get-go, the smells found in this lot from DiRahmah set a very high standard for wet-hulled coffee. The grounds have a scent of brown rice syrup with some wood spice hints and a note of salted plum. The aroma is rustic and sweet, burned caramel pungency, cherry wood and a savory butter note folded in. The brew is a real treat. It has the herbal and earthy notes you might expect from a wet-hulled coffee. But along with that come top notes that are uncharacteristic for this type of process. Acidity, for one, is quite present when the coffee is roasted to City-City+ and adds a sort of black tea/tannic affect on the cup profile. I welcome it, as Sumatras are often some of the most complex and earth-toned coffees we carry, and I feel the acidic impression helps to tie together some of the disparate flavor notes. Middle roasts have more of the intense bittersweetness, that when coupled with a foresty sweet flavor, culminates in something like fine bean-to-bar chocolate. Top notes are all over the place with this coffee and really depend on where your roast winds up. But look for hints of goji berry, sandalwood, Thai basil, anise and pipe tobacco. Brew tip: if you want a brighter Sumatra cup, try a higher water-to-coffee brew ratio. I've settled on 17g water:1g coffee coffee and am really enjoying DiRahmah's slight 'bright' side without sacrificing body.</p> </div>
