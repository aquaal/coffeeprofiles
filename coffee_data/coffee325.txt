<div class="column-right">
<p>Carlos Apio is a small-holder farm in the Bajo Belen, a village in Colombia's Inzá, Cauca region. "Bajo" as you may have guessed, refers to the lower part of Belen, which at 1800 meters, is far from a low altitude zone! This is a wet-processed coffee, most farmers using old style hand-cranked pulpers or small motor-run machines, fermenting and washing in the same tank (the first pic is of a dual-use tank), and then drying out on raised, covered beds. Most farms have a healthy amount of Caturra planted, as well as some Timor hybrids like Variedad Colombia and Tabi as a way to protect against coffee leaf rust ("La Roya"). Carlos' coffee has the fruited flavor attributes of a coffee that underwent long fermentation time. It's not uncommon for small farmers in this region to mix harvest days in the fermentation tank. What this means is, the 1st batch in will ferment longer than the ones from the following day (or days). At one time, this was considered a "no no" for fear of overfermentation. But what we're seeing is that changing out the water in the tank with fresh, cold water regularly helps to prevent fermentation from going too far, slowing things down a bit. For the small farmer, it allows them to process small batches of coffee from multiple days together (usually 2 or 3) in one lot rather than managing a bunch of tiny lots on the drying patio. It's not a new practice and adds to the wide range of flavors found in these regions.</p>

<p>Cupping this next to a more muted, basic coffee, really brought out the fruited side of Carlos Apio's coffee. I pick up on a dehyderated fruit smell that covers banana and apricot at the very least, with some brown sugar and a touch of old-fashioned bitters. The wet aroma is inviting to say the least, smells of cooked sugar and fruit reduction are lifted in the steam. When roasted light (City/City+), the cup is incredibly sweet and pushes fruit pectin-like flavors about as far as they can go for a wet-processed coffee. The initial sugar and coffee bittering flavors quickly fade to heavy-handed notes of fleshy fruits, jam and fruit compotes. Adjectives that come to mind are plum jelly, dried fig, sweetened cranberry juice and more. I imagine the fruit-forward cup qualities might be polarizing for some, as this coffee is far from "restrained". But the profile is backed by high level of sweetness, which is crucial in keeping fruit flavors from coming off like vinegar. Full City roasting brings out more of a cooked berry note and bittersweet cacao bar quickly finds its way into full view.</p> </div>
