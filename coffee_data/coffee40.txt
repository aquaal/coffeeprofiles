<div class="column-right">
<p>Dusangirijambo Coop is perched on Mururui hill in Sogestal Kayanza, northern Burundi. The cooperative was built about 6 years ago, and serves farmer members in the Mururi area through buying and processing the local coffee. We came across there coffee 2 years ago via a cooperative union in the region. The union helps the individual regional coops with everything from building coffee washing stations, providing agronomical support, preparing coffee for export at a central dry milling facility, and eventually linking the finished product to an international market. Dusangirijambo is outfitted with a de-pulping station where they receive daily deliveries from the surrounding small farmers. The coffees are de-pulped, fermented and soaked under water, then laid to dry on raised wire beds that help facilitate airflow and even-drying as a result. The station itself sits at 1650 meters above sea level, and the high part of the hill we drove in on peaked at 1880 meters.</p> </div>

<p>Smells of baking spices and bittersweetness are released in the ground coffee, whole clove and all-spice notes accenting raw sugar and caramel smells. The wet aroma takes these smells to the next level, reaching an apex of caramelizing sugar and spiced sweetness that has elements of coffee cake crust. Light roasting yields a cup that has black tea-like characteristics amidst sugar browning sweetness and tannic bittersweetness. As it cools, a soft apple note provides contrast and acidity is brisk, also characteristic of black tea. But trust me, we are talking about coffee, and it's the balance of sweet and bitter flavors - core coffee characteristics - that define this coffee's essence. For me, City+ is a near perfect cup, layered sweetness giving way to subtle baking spice accents, and acidity that leaves a lemon-like impression on your palate. It's not overly bright/citric, but more like the understated tartness of water with a spritz of lemon. At Full City you get a complex and rich dark chocolate/cherry flavor that reminds me of those chocolate covered dried whole cherries, and is particularly prevalent when brewing as espresso. </p> </div>
