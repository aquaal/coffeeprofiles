<div class="column-right">
<p>This small 3 bag lot of honey process coffee from a producer named Marselina Walu. Not only is she a small grower in Bajawa area of Flores, but she's also the leader of a small farmer's cooperative in the area. Much of the kinship groups in these areas are matrilineal, with land being passed through women. Because of this, you see a lot more women like Marselina in leadership positions at the farm and cooperative levels. This is an all female-run coop and we were introduced to their coffee through a Q-grader we're working with in the region. Most of the coffees are processed on hand cranked or motorized depulping machines on the farm, fermentation in buckets or small tanks, and then drying on raised drying beds. The soil is rich in nutrients, and they are able to grow coffee using fully organic farm practices, though they are not certified at this time. This is a honey-process, which means much of the fruit is left intact with the seed during the drying phase. Unlike fully washed coffee, where all of the fruit is removed immediately after depulping (removing the cherry), honey processing tends to impart fruit flavors on the cup profile, add body and round off acidity. We certainly tasted some of this affect on Marselina's coffee, though I think it's still far from "fruit-forward". One other affect of honey processing is that the coffee has more silver skin and produces more chaff when roasting. Something to keep in mind when judging roast color during roasting as well as keeping an eye on the chaff collection system to make sure it is free of excessive chaff.</p> </div>

<p>The cup has sweetness like palm sugar that blankets the coffee in a rustic-sweet overlay, leading to hints of natural dried fruit and complex aromatics. Light roasts have a pungent sweet smell that comes off like cake batter with some fruited character that's on the low-intensity side, but still perceptible (especially in light roasts). One of my roasts was Full City and it was apparent from the smells that that's just too dark for this coffee. The coffee weighs heavy on the palate, even at City, with a mouthfeel like fruit juice. Marselina's coffee has apple-like brightness that adds to the fruited cup character. I noticed a few quakers in my roasted coffee, mostly partial-quakers that on their own in small amount, shouldn't affect the cup. But in high concentration they impart dry nut flavors and are worth picking out the most obvious offenders by hand. You can spot them by their light tan shade that is unchanged by roast degree. It's worth the extra effort to get the most out of this interesting and complex coffee, that at 180 total lbs, will unfortunately be a flash in the pan.</p> </div>
