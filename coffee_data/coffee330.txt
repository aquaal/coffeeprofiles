<div class="column-right">
<p>This blend of coffee from Inzá is made up of coffees from 5 neighboring producers in Vereda San Antonio, "vereda" being the equivalent of a small neighborhood. This time around we have 19 bags in total and I think the largest contribution from a singe producer was 7 bags or parchment coffee. The province of Inzá is located in Southwestern Colombia within the greater Department of Cauca. As you make the drive from La Plata to Inzá, you follow the Rio Paez, and an eventual crossing over a suspension bridge lands you on the road to the the villages whose coffees make up this blend. Like much of Colombia, Cauca is home to some very high altitude farms, many breaching the 2000 meter mark, the coffee from this lot harvested from an altitude range of about 1600 to 2000 meters. The way we make up these regional blends is by cupping several samples from the individual farms, separating out those that meet a certain cup criteria, and then blending them together. It's a great benefit to us (and not to mention the cup) having this level of quality control with our Colombian blends. This is a wet-processed coffee, most farmers using old style hand-cranked pulpers, fermenting and washing in the same tank, and then drying out on raised, covered beds. Most farms have a healthy amount of Caturra planted, as well as some Timor hybrids (like Variedad Colombia and Tabi) in response to the major leaf rust outbreak in the 1980's. </p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>The dry fragrance has a brown sugar sweetness with subtle herb or spice note that brings to mind cardamom. City+ roasts bring out dark spice and cookie-like sweetness (think shortbread). This coffee from San Antonio farmers makes for a very sweet cup at a wide range of roasts, City and City+ boasting more of the raw panela sugar type sweetness and cut through by fruited acidity. There's a shift in flavors as you continue drinking. Accents of dehydrated raisin and date pieces are interspersed throughout and a spiced note caps off the finishing flavors. The sweetness reaches a fairly intense level, even at Full City with a note of vanilla caramels and a bittersweet low tones. But that's not all, San Antonio displays a thin layer of dark fruit sandwiched between the bittersweet core, presenting a nice option for espresso too.</p> </div>
