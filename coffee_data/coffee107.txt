<div class="column-right">
<p>While we find ourselves locating great quality coffee from the same wet-mill sources in Kenya, it's exciting when a new name (at least to us) crops up on the cupping table, and is associated with an outstanding cup! That's the case this year with this lot of Gatuya AA. It hails from Gatuya region in Kenya's Murang'a district within the Central Province. Gatuya is a "coffee factory", the Kenyan name for a wet-mill that processes coffee cherry. In every practical sense, it functions as a farmer's cooperative in that it is made up of and governed by farmer members. Gatuya is part of the New Murarandia Farmers Cooperative Society ("FCS"), which serves over 3,500 farmer members in total, most of which have less than half a hectare of coffee planted. The farmers selling coffee to Mutitu are growing a mix of SL-28, SL-34, Ruiru-11 and Batian cultivars, and most farms fall between 1800 and 1900 meters above sea level. This is the AA outturn, the largest bean size in Kenya coffee grades and commands the highest price. Every process batch is made up of a fairly wide range of different sized beans, and in Kenya these sizes are grouped together by range measured in 1/16ths of an inch. These different classifications are generally referred to as "outturns", the most common being "AA" = 17-19 1/64", "AB" = 15-17 1/64", and peaberry.</p> </div>

<p>In so many ways this AA Gatuya has what we consider "classic" Kenya cup characteristics. The dry fragrance is thick with brown sugar sweetness that is spiced by flecks of cardamom and clove. I pick up on a wine-like grape note when pouring hot water to the grounds and a sweetness that smells saturated and intense. The cup shows many of the flavor characteristics that come to mind when I think of Kenya coffee: black currant, raw sugar sweetness, orange peel and a bright, citric acidity. It's delicious when roasted light and medium and I found the fruit flavors shift quite a bit with roast level. City roasts are sure to be the most vivid in terms of acidity, intense with citrus flavors like grapefruit that illicit near-puckering brightness. The fruit accents at City+ and Full City, however, are more like dark berry and black currant, darker fruits that are sweet and marked by a hint of wood spice. Don't get me wrong, middle roasts are so much brighter than your average brew but the acidity is more integrated into the fruit flavors, the sharpness rounded off a bit. I brewed my City+ roast over a weekend and just loved drinking it!</p> </div>
