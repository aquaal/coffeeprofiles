<div class="column-right">
<p>David Garcia Diaz has a little less than a hectare of coffee in the San Francisco village of San Jose del Alto, Jaen. This 1800 meter farm is one of two plots David manages and is planted in Bourbon and Caturra. We noticed a unique fruited quality in the cup and figured there must be longer fermentation at play. Sure enough, David is doing a sort of "pre-fermentation" of the coffee cherry in bags before depulping and washing. This lends to some of the more pectin-like flavors that are found in the brew. The coffee is dried on raised beds for up to 2 weeks depending on weather. The group who coordinated this buying project in Jaen are working to identify farmers who consistently produce good quality coffee (like David's), offering to cash premiums based on quality tiers. They register all of the farmers they buy from and track payments from the initial base payout to the 2nd payments that are delivered after the coffee finds a home. </p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>City roasts show some fruited sweetness in the dry fragrance, that along with a caramel note, come together like caramel-covered apple. The wet aroma has honey sweetness at City+, paired with something like warmed oatmeal with brown sugar. I found the fruited character to be most pronounced in the cup - which is where it <em>really</em> counts, right? That said, "fruit" is not the central cup characteristic in my assessment, but rather, raw sugar flavors are. The underlying, unrefined sweetness of demerara and sugar-in-the-raw gives way to a baked apple flavor that straddles a sweet/winey edge and seems to build some steam in the aftertaste. The coffee is transformative in that way, starting off candy sweet and fading to hints of fruit in aroma. The cool cup also presents a sweet walnut note that tastes like it underwent a dusting of cocoa powder. Full City roasts manage to have a resonant fruited side too - dark grape in this case - and with it comes bittersweet flavors that echo chocolate torte.</p> </div>
