<div class="column-right">
<p>Ibutiti Estate is a privately owned farm by the Murathe family, located in the Kiria-ini area of Murang'a County. The Murathe's have roughly 40 hectares of coffee planted, mostly SL bourbon types. We had a chance to tour the farm and milling facility onsite a couple years ago and they run an impressive operation. Their washing station is in good order, tidy and organized, as is their farm. The trees are well spaced and pruned, and appeared to be out-producing others we visited in the area, no doubt as a result of agronomical care. This is the AA outturn, and we also picked up a peaberry selection too which sold out in about a day! Both show impressive fruit flavors, with enough acidity to structure cup complexity. The physical quality of the green measures out nicely too, and I did not see any full quaker beans in the few hundred grams I roasted, and only a few hollow beans ("shells") which don't have an affect on cup flavor in small number. I'm recommending to roast Ibutiti light in order to capture complexity at its apex.</p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>"Really nice spiced orange tea" were the first notes I jotted down when smelling the ground coffee. And I stand by that description as the fruit-to-spice characteristics of this AA lot are hard to miss and are incredibly inticing. The aroma at City roast level is rife with a brown sugar sweetness, as well as a strong grape smell in the steam that's accented by a mix of complex baking spices. Roast tones are kept in check when sticking to the recommended roast range ofCity - City+ and still manage to develop big sweetness, bouyant fruited flavors and aroma in the brewed coffee. Characteristics of mulled fruit juices like grape and orange come through in light roasts, and the finish is marked by accents of cardamom, all-spice and clove. There's a juiciness in weight that along with a tart berry note comes across like mixed berry juice. Berry-like brightness adds vibrance to the cup profile and comes off like cranberry tea with lemon. If you missed the fruit-forward peaberry selection that was a flash in the pan last week, here's your chance to pull a pristine big-bean version from the same harvest lot.</p> </div>
