<div class="column-right">
<p>Comasagua is a town in El Salvador's La Libertad Department, tucked away in the El Balsamo mountain range. The altitude ranges from around 1000 meters to as high as 1500, and it's in this watershed region that the San Antonio coffee cooperative was erected some 40 years ago. Through the years, the coop has remained focused on sustainable agronomical practices with members using organic fertilizers and minimizing the use of synthetic inputs, and planting coffee in ways that help ground permeability, water retention, and decrease soil erosion. The coop started using their own eco-pulper for processing coffee down to the parchment-covered seed and drying on cement patios. The coffee is then trucked down to a dry mill nearby where the coffee can be stored in a cool warehouse space, and eventually, the parchment layer removed in order to prepare for export. Patio space for drying coffee is something the cooperative has struggled with during the peak of the harvest and we, tacked on a little extra to the purchase of this coffee lot to help fund patio expansion for next year. We weren't able to travel to San Antonio due to the pandemic, but hope to make a first trip next harvest.</p> </div>

<p>What the dry fragrance lacks in sweetness and clarity, the wet aromatics make up for. You don't have to be close to the cup to smell the aroma either. Caramel sweetness comes up in the steam, along with bits and pieces of candied nut, caramelized sugars and something lactic, like warm milk. Light roasts have almond and toffee tastes in the brew along with an herbal hint in the finish. The lighter roasts offer an acidic impression that was missing in darker roasts. Full City roasts are full on chocolate bittersweet, low tones reverberant, and body so thick you could cut it with a knife (or cupping spoon!). A perfect Central American coffee option for milk drinks and espresso.</p> </div>
