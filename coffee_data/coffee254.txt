<div class="column-right">
<p>This is the honey-process lot from "Paso Ancho", an estate located high on the hillside above the large, well known Finca La Florentina. In fact, La Florentina used to buy the coffee cherry from all the surrounding farms to augment their own. La Florentina is down in the flat valley and Paso Ancho is roughly another 500 meters higher up, on a very steep hillside with southern exposure. Due to the high altitude, the coffee has greater density, and we often taste better acidity, a more piquant cup than the coffee from some of the lower grown estates. The farm is connected to it's own, independent micro-mill down in the valley and uses the de-mucilage process where the mucilage is stripped off the parchment layer using friction, rather than traditional fermentation. I was very impressed with the high altitude and excellent practices when visiting several years back and can only imagine the improvements they've made since. The entire farm is very high altitude; it starts at 1750 meters, an altitude many farms don't even reach, and goes up from there to as high as 1900! Altitude matters with coffee, and you can taste the difference here. Altitude allows coffee to ripen more slowly, creates greater bean density, and results in higher concentrations of bright, snappy, acidity in the cup. We also have a <a href="https://www.sweetmarias.com/panama-dry-process-paso-anch-6569.html">Dry Process</a> lot available, and both wet process and anaerobic fermentation batches on their way!</p> </div>

<div class="column-right">
<p>Not only is this lot from Paso Ancho honey-processed, but the sweetness found in the cup shows honey flavor characteristics too. Big body and mild acidity are notable, and when you brew this next to the wet process lot from the same farm, you can taste how process has impacted the cup. The hot brew at City+ produces a raw honey that extends to sweetness, along with some mild bittering tones. The cup is balanced, and is surely a good single origin (SO) espresso option, Full City roasts developing more of a bittersweet cocoa flavor profile. As the brew of my lighter roast cooled down, subtle hints of toasted grains like barley and wheat berry came up from the honey-sweet base, along with hints of black tea, golden raisin, and a flavor of sesame seed/sugar candies.</p> </div>
