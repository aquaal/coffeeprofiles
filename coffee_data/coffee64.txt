<div class="column-right">
<p>This is a unique year for Ethiopian coffee exports as it's the first where farmers like Aba Nura were given the opportunity to export their own coffee. In previous years, all private operations big and small had to sell their coffee through the government-run Ethiopia Commodity Exchange and only cooperatives and unions were exempt. That probably sounds worse than it really was as government pricing and payments were actually stringently tracked and traceable. But an issue presented to the buyer is that ECX coffee was stripped of provenance, save origin, so we never knew where it came from. This farmer's coffee, for example, would've just been blended off with other coffees of a similar grade and sold as "Limu Grade 1". All that has changed and he is one of many farmers who have successfully applied for export licenses and are able to sell directly. His farm and small wet mill are located in Bashasha, not far from Agaro town. The farm rests at just over 2000 meters above sea level and is 18 hectares in size. Aba Nura is one of a few single-farmer lots we were lucky enough to export directly this year. We hope to repeat this business in 2020!</p> </div>

<p>Both the fragrance and wet aromatics have berry character that comes through like pastry filling when roasted light (I pick up on strawberry), chocolate-covered berry at Full City. There's a smell in the wetted grounds that reminded me of ripe cherimoya, which if you haven't smelled or tasted one before, has a pungent aroma that's something like a cross of banana and papaya. The flavors in the brewed coffee show dried fruited character, unrefined sugar-in-the-raw sweetness and chocolate-malted grains. The coffee cools to discernible flavors of blackberry and dried blueberry intermixed with chocolate flavor notes that fade to an aftertaste of dark cocoa powder. The coffee is sweet and relatively clean. Not just in relation to other natural-process Ethiopias (which it <em>is</em>), but also considering it's from the tail end of the last harvest. Acidity is mild, but pleasant and manages to prop up the complex fruited cup some. My roast that was just shy of 2nd crack showed balanced bittersweetness, with dark berry and prune flavors notes too. Dry process coffees produce a lot more chaff than washed, and I recommend Behmor users keep batch size to 200g or less. I found that 150 gram roasts move along at a steady clip and did not encounter much in the way of chaff ignition on the heating elements.</p> </div>
